What Are Candles? A Journey from Ancient Flames to Modern Art

🕯️ 7 min read📅 June 2026✍️ Nature Nest Team

At their simplest, candles are a source of light — a wick surrounded by wax that, when lit, produces a steady flame. But candles are also so much more: they're vessels of memory, anchors of ritual, instruments of relaxation, and increasingly, objects of art. The global candle market was valued at over $11 billion in 2024 — and it's still growing — driven by a cultural shift toward wellness, self-care, and the desire to make our living spaces feel like sanctuaries.

A Brief History of Candles

Ancient Beginnings (3000 BCE – 500 CE)

The earliest known candles were made by the Ancient Egyptians, who created rushlights — reeds soaked in melted animal fat (tallow). These weren't true wicked candles, but they served the same purpose. The Romans are generally credited with developing the first wicked candles, dipping rolled papyrus into melted tallow or beeswax. These early candles were primarily functional — used for lighting homes, guiding travelers at night, and religious ceremonies.

The Middle Ages: Beeswax Revolution

In medieval Europe, beeswax candles emerged as a superior alternative to tallow. They burned cleaner, brighter, and without the acrid smoke that made tallow candles unpleasant. However, beeswax was expensive — reserved for churches, monasteries, and the wealthy. For ordinary people, tallow candles (made from rendered beef or mutton fat) remained the norm, and their foul smell was a daily nuisance.

The 19th Century: Industrialization Changes Everything

The 1800s brought two revolutionary changes to candle-making. First, in the 1820s, French chemist Michel Eugène Chevreul discovered how to separate fatty acids from glycerin, leading to stearin — a hard, durable wax that burned cleanly. Second, in the 1850s, paraffin wax was discovered as a byproduct of petroleum refining. Paraffin was cheap, burned well, and could be produced at industrial scale — suddenly, candles were affordable for everyone. The invention of the continuous wick-making machine in 1834 further mechanized production, and candles became a commodity.

Electricity & the Candle's Reinvention (20th Century)

When electric lighting became widespread in the early 1900s, the candle industry faced obsolescence. But candles didn't disappear — they transformed. No longer needed for basic illumination, candles became decorative, ceremonial, and aromatic. The 1980s and 90s saw an explosion of interest in scented candles as the wellness movement gained momentum. Candles became a lifestyle product — something you chose, not something you needed.

How a Candle Actually Works

The science of a candle is elegant in its simplicity — a self-sustaining cycle of heat, fuel, and combustion:

  1. The wick is lit — The flame's heat melts a small pool of wax near the wick base.
  2. Capillary action draws the liquid wax up through the wick — like water climbing a paper towel.
  3. Vaporization — The heat of the flame vaporizes the liquid wax into a gas.
  4. Combustion — The wax vapor mixes with oxygen and burns, producing the flame you see.
  5. The cycle continues — The flame's heat melts more wax, which travels up the wick, and the cycle repeats until the wax is gone.

This is why the wick matters so much — too thick and the candle smokes; too thin and the flame drowns in melted wax. At Nature Nest, we use lead-free cotton wicks sized specifically for each candle's wax blend and diameter, ensuring a clean, even burn every time.

Types of Candles You'll Encounter

The modern candle comes in many forms — each with its own character:

  • Container/Jar Candles — Wax poured directly into a glass, ceramic, or tin vessel. Most common for scented candles. Safe, clean-burning, and reusable.
  • Pillar Candles — Freestanding, self-supporting candles. Often unscented, used for decoration or dining.
  • Taper Candles — Tall, thin candles designed to fit into holders. Classic dinner table candles.
  • Tealights — Small candles in thin metal or plastic cups. Great for ambiance and oil warmers.
  • Votive Candles — Small, squat candles meant to be burned in a tight-fitting holder where they liquefy completely.
  • Floating Candles — Designed to float on water. Stunning for centerpieces and events.
  • Sculptural/Art Candles — Candles shaped into artistic forms — flowers, figures, abstract geometries. Burnable art.

Why Handmade Candles Are Different

A factory-made paraffin candle and a handmade soy-and-beeswax candle are fundamentally different products. Handmade candles use better ingredients, burn longer and cleaner, and carry the signature of the person who made them. There's an intentionality — from the way the wax is poured at exactly the right temperature to the placement of the wick for an even melt pool. When you buy handmade, you're buying hundreds of small decisions made by someone who cares about the result.
