What Are Candles? A Journey from Ancient Flames to Modern Art

🕯️ 7 min read📅 June 2026✍️ Nature Nest Team

A candle is the simplest thing. Wax, a wick, a flame. Light it, and a room changes. But we all know they're more than that. A candle is what you light when you want to slow down. What you buy when words aren't enough for a gift. What you reach for when the day was long and you need something — anything — that just feels good. The global candle market hit $11 billion in 2024 and is still climbing. Not because people suddenly need fire. Because people need moments of calm, and a candle is the cheapest form of peace you can buy.

A Brief History of Candles

Ancient Beginnings (3000 BCE – 500 CE)

The Egyptians got there first. They made rushlights — reeds dipped in animal fat — which weren't exactly candles, but close enough. The Romans figured out the wick, dipping rolled papyrus into melted tallow or beeswax. These early candles were purely practical: light the house, guide travelers, hold ceremonies. No one was buying them for the vibes.

The Middle Ages: Beeswax Changes Everything

Beeswax candles showed up in medieval Europe and they were a revelation. Cleaner. Brighter. No acrid smoke that made your eyes water. But they were expensive. Only churches, monasteries, and rich households could afford them. Everyone else burned tallow — rendered beef or mutton fat — and it smelled about as good as that sounds.

The 19th Century: Candles Go Industrial

Two discoveries changed everything. In the 1820s, a French chemist named Michel Eugène Chevreul figured out how to extract stearin from animal fats — a hard, clean-burning wax. Then in the 1850s, someone realized paraffin, a petroleum byproduct, made great candles. It was cheap. It burned well. Factories could churn it out by the ton. Candles became something everyone could afford.

Electricity Arrives, Candles Reinvent Themselves

When electric light swept through homes in the early 1900s, the candle industry should have died. Instead, candles transformed. They stopped being about light and became about atmosphere. Decor. Ceremony. Scent. The 1980s and 90s brought the wellness wave, and suddenly everyone wanted a candle that smelled like a spa. Candles went from necessity to luxury, from utility to identity. That's still the world we're in today.

How a Candle Actually Works

It's a loop. Simple and brilliant:

  1. You light the wick. The flame melts a tiny pool of wax at the base.
  2. The wick pulls that liquid wax upward — same way water climbs a paper towel. Capillary action.
  3. The heat vaporizes the wax. Liquid becomes gas.
  4. That gas burns. Mixes with oxygen, produces the flame you see.
  5. Repeat. The flame melts more wax, the wick pulls it up, and the cycle keeps going until there's nothing left.

This is why wick size matters so much. Too thick and your candle smokes. Too thin and the flame drowns in melted wax. We use lead-free cotton wicks, sized specifically for each candle's wax type and jar diameter. It's a small thing most people never think about. But it's the difference between a candle that burns perfectly and one that doesn't.

Types of Candles You'll See

Not all candles are built the same. Here's what's out there:

  • Jar Candles — Wax poured into a glass, ceramic, or tin container. Most scented candles are jar candles. Safe, clean, reusable.
  • Pillars — Freestanding and sturdy. Usually unscented. Great for decoration or dinner tables.
  • Tapers — Tall and thin. The classic dinner candle that needs a holder.
  • Tealights — Tiny candles in little metal cups. Ambience in miniature.
  • Votives — Short and squat. They melt completely, so they need a tight holder.
  • Floating Candles — They float on water. Killer for centerpieces and events.
  • Sculptural Candles — Shaped like flowers, figures, abstract forms. Art you can burn.

Why Handmade Is Different

A factory paraffin candle and a handmade soy-and-beeswax candle are not the same product. They just look similar. Handmade means better wax. Longer burn. Cleaner flame. But more than that — handmade means someone actually decided how hot to pour, where to place the wick, how long to cure it. Hundreds of tiny decisions, made by a person who cares what happens when you light it.
